A plate of area 1 m2 whose normal makes an angle θ with incident radiation actually presents an effective area of ‘Cos θ’ to the radiation. This relationship is called the cosine law which says that for the same area, a plate inclined at an angle would receive smaller radiant flux than that compared to plate normal to the radiant beam. Hence the flux on a plate of unit area at the surface of the earth will be 1000 Cos θ W/m2, where θ is the angle that normal to the side of the plate we are interested in makes with respect to the line joining the plate to sun. When -90 < θ < 90 the plate will recieve some (>0) radiation on the side we are interested in. When -90 > θ > -180 OR 90 < θ < 180 radiation is actually falling on the side opposite to the one we are interested in

Let us understand the role that some of the parameters play in deciding how much radiation falls on a plate 1 m2 in area at the surface of the Earth.

Consider the plate whose normal is along the suns radiation. Consider an axis system where x-axis and y-axis are on the surface of the plate and z-axis is along the normal to the plate. If the plate is now rotated through an angle about y-axis OR x-axis, the amount of radiation falling on it will be affected by Cosine of the amount of rotation. But a rotation about z-axis will not affect the radiation falling on it.
